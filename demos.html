How it works

The pinhole camera. A camera is, to first approximation, a single point that light passes through onto a sensor. The intrinsic matrix K captures the lens geometry: the focal length f (in pixels) sets how strongly the scene is magnified, and the principal point (cₓ, cᵧ) is where the optical axis pierces the image. Inverting K turns a pixel back into a direction in the camera's own frame: dcam = K⁻¹[u, v, 1]ᵀ. That is a ray, not a point — a single image can't tell you how far along that ray the world actually is.

Pose, and the trick that recovers depth. The extrinsics place the camera in the world: here, a height h above the floor and a downward pitch φ. Rotating the ray by R(φ) expresses it in world coordinates. To pin down distance from one image, I add one assumption — the thing I clicked lies on the ground plane (Z = 0). The ray then has exactly one ground intersection, found by scaling it until its height reaches zero: λ = −h / dworld,z, giving the 3D point P = C + λdworld. The forward and lateral parts of P are the real-world position of that pixel.

Why distance hurts. Pixels above the horizon (vᵢ = cᵧ − f·tanφ) point at or above the plane and never meet it — the demo reports no intersection there. Just below the horizon, a one-pixel move sweeps the ray across enormous distances, so range explodes and a single pixel of detection error becomes metres of position error. The "depth sensitivity" read-out is exactly that: how many metres of range one pixel of vertical error buys at the point you clicked. Click near your feet, then near the horizon, and watch it blow up. That sensitivity is the whole reason a real robot can't lean on flat-ground monocular depth at range.

What the production system actually did. ICURO's platform didn't assume a flat floor for depth. It recovered the depth scale from a moving camera — multi-frame triangulation through a VSLAM map, with no depth sensor — and fed those 3D waypoints to operators. This demo isolates the foundational special case so the camera model itself is easy to see; the EKF rebuild it grew into (occupancy-ekf-fusion) carries the multi-sensor fusion and uses the same camera intrinsics the defaults here come from (f = 500, principal point 320×240, on a 640×480 image). The pinhole math is in-browser and self-contained; the repo is Python, so it isn't run live here.

Interactive · a neural-network intuition

One neuron per letter

The smallest honest picture of how a neural network classifies. Draw a letter on the 7×5 grid by clicking pixels. Each candidate letter is a single output neuron whose weights are a stored template; its activation is just how well your drawing lines up with that template, and the strongest neuron wins. "Teach" overwrites a neuron's weights with whatever you've drawn — learning, made visible.

How it works

The grid is 35 binary inputs — one number per pixel, on or off. Each letter is one output neuron, and its 35 weights are a little picture of that letter. The activation is a weighted sum: pixels that agree with the template push the score up, pixels that disagree push it down. Whichever neuron scores highest is the prediction, and the bars show every neuron's activation so you can see the runner-up and why a sloppy stroke flips the answer.

Pressing "Teach" sets the selected neuron's weights to your current drawing. That is the entire idea behind training, stripped of machinery: learning is choosing weights so the right neuron fires hardest. A real OCR model stacks many neurons across several layers and learns those weights from thousands of examples by gradient descent rather than by copying one drawing — but the one-layer version is where the intuition lives, and it's the version I reach for first with students.
