Compositional Estimation Under Occlusion (in progress)

Temporal aggregation · allocation with abstention · github ↗

A method for estimating the per-class composition of a falling stream of mixed objects — say a bin of produce tipped out all at once — when the items heavily occlude one another. The question I find genuinely interesting: given only a cluttered, self-occluding video and a known total, how much of each class is actually there?

Problem

A pile at rest is maximally occluded — items hide behind each other and no single view sees the whole. Any one frame undercounts, and which classes it undercounts changes frame to frame. A system that reports confident per-class numbers from a single frame will be confidently wrong.

Approach

The fall itself is the opportunity. As objects tumble they separate, so the falling stream is a moving cross-section: an item hidden at one instant is visible at another. Tracking objects through the fall and accumulating per-class evidence weighted by how clearly each was seen recovers a composition no single frame contains.

Two ideas carry it. When a measured total is available — a scale reading, for instance — vision only needs to estimate each class's fraction; multiplying by the known total normalizes out a whole family of absolute-estimation errors. And when occlusion makes part of the stream genuinely ambiguous, the right output is not a confident guess but an explicit unattributed residual: so many units of this, so many of that, and a named remainder the system won't pretend to know. Confidently misattributed is a worse failure than honestly unattributed, and the design treats it that way.

Status

The simulation environment and tracking are in place; the evidence-aggregation and allocation logic, the failure analysis, and the results are in progress.
