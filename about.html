About

Building perception systems for the physical world

I build computer vision and perception systems that survive contact with the real world — messy data, edge hardware, and operators who need the system to work, not score well on benchmarks. Across my work I have an evaluation-first approach: define what "correct" actually means for the problem before reaching for a solution, then hold the system to that definition honestly.

Places I've built things (and made friends)

ICURO — Founding Autonomous Systems Engineer. My strongest anchor in perception and robotics. I built a multi-sensor perception and localization stack for a teleoperated platform: a four-sensor suite fusing camera VSLAM, fiducial pose, sonar, and 2D LiDAR through an EKF with confidence-weighted failover, plus a probabilistic occupancy grid consumed by the planner. I shipped edge vision on Jetson with a roughly 70% latency reduction, and the systems I built there reached daily production use by hundreds of operators.

Applied Materials — Machine Vision Engineer. My evaluation-discipline anchor. I solved an SEM defect-detection and clustering problem that three prior engineering teams had failed to solve — not with a bigger model, but by defining domain-grounded correctness metrics first, then building to them. I documented the approach in an internal IEEE-style paper.

Kimberly-Clark — AI Tech Lead. Several years of production ML discipline across forecasting, price elasticity, and promotion optimization. I authored the first-generation elasticity model and the forecasting pipeline myself — the forecasting work through change management with business stakeholders — then led delivery, production rollout, and generalization across markets and product categories. I led and mentored the rotating team of engineers and data scientists who extended that work, and instituted PSI-based drift monitoring with automated retraining. I trust causal-confounder control over naive curve-fitting, and a drift trigger over a hopeful deploy.

Independent (2026–present). Client projects in classical 3D perception: a scan-to-BIM reconstruction pipeline converting LiDAR scans of buildings into structured IFC models, and an embedded road-defect detection and navigation system — alongside the open-source perception work in my projects.

How I like to work

I care about difficult physical-world ML problems and the unglamorous engineering that makes a system survivable in production — the part most demos skip. I trust production usability and operator reliability more than headline model metrics. Geometry and classical methods where they're enough, learning where geometry runs out. Away from the screen I'm usually on a basketball court, behind a camera, carving wood, or cooking something elaborate with my wife.

Teaching

From 2021 to 2025 I designed and taught AI for Robotics (AISV 807) at UC Santa Cruz Silicon Valley Extension — an applied course covering perception, SLAM fundamentals, and deployment pipelines, with real-time detection projects and student capstones I mentored, including a vision-based robotic-arm grasping system. Teaching has helped me stay close to my fundamentals and keep learning — students have a remarkable talent for asking the questions you weren't expecting.

Education & foundations

B.S. in Computer Engineering, California State University, Sacramento (2018), Tau Beta Pi. Primary languages Python and C++; deep work in 3D scene understanding, visual SLAM and structure from motion, multi-view geometry, semantic segmentation, and edge deployment (ONNX, TensorRT, CUDA, Jetson).
